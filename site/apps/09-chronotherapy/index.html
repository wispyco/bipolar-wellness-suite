Light therapy, dark therapy, and wake therapy scheduling based on ISBD chronotherapy recommendations. These interventions target the circadian system to stabilize mood.

Bright Light Therapy Scheduler

Bright light therapy is recommended by the ISBD for bipolar depression. Proper timing is critical -- morning light can help depression, but mid-day or evening light may trigger mania.

Within 30 min of waking for depression
10,000 lux is the clinical standard

Safety Guidelines

  • Start with 15 min and increase gradually
  • Use in the morning only -- afternoon/evening use can trigger mania
  • Stop immediately if you notice mania or hypomania symptoms
  • Do not look directly at the light source
  • Consult your psychiatrist before starting, especially if on lithium
  • Monitor mood daily during the first 2 weeks

Your Weekly Schedule

Dark Therapy / Blue Light Management

Dark therapy limits light exposure in the evening to support melatonin production and circadian alignment. For acute mania, extended darkness (14 hours) has shown rapid anti-manic effects.

Evening Routine (6pm onward)

  • 6:00 PM -- Dim overhead lights, switch to warm/amber lighting
  • 7:00 PM -- Activate blue light filters on all screens (Night Shift, f.lux)
  • 8:00 PM -- Wear amber/orange-tinted glasses if still using screens
  • 9:00 PM -- Reduce screen use to minimum; prefer paper books or audio
  • 10:00 PM -- Complete darkness in bedroom; blackout curtains recommended

Acute Mania Protocol (14-hour darkness)

  • Dark period: 6:00 PM to 8:00 AM (14 hours)
  • Complete darkness or amber-only lighting
  • Must be supervised by psychiatrist
  • Can produce rapid improvement in manic symptoms
  • Virtual darkness (amber glasses) is an alternative to total darkness

Wake Therapy (Sleep Deprivation Therapy)

Total or partial sleep deprivation can produce rapid antidepressant effects in bipolar depression. This must be done under clinical supervision.

Total Wake Therapy

Stay awake for 36 consecutive hours (one full night plus the following day).

Day 1, 7:00 AMWake as normal
Day 1, all dayStay active, bright light exposure in morning
Night 1Stay awake through the night (no naps)
Day 2, all dayContinue staying awake, bright light in morning
Day 2, 7:00 PMGo to bed early for recovery sleep

Partial Wake Therapy (Late Night)

Sleep from 9 PM to 1 AM, then stay awake until the next evening. Often better tolerated.

Triple Chronotherapy

Combines wake therapy + bright light therapy + sleep phase advance. The most potent protocol.

Night 1Total wake therapy (stay up all night)
Day 2 morning30 min bright light therapy
Day 2, 6:00 PMRecovery sleep (early bedtime)
Days 3-7Gradually shift bedtime later by 1 hour each night
Day 7+Maintain target sleep schedule + daily light therapy

Critical Safety Notes

  • Wake therapy MUST be supervised by a psychiatrist
  • Can trigger mania or mixed states in some patients
  • Do not attempt if you have a history of rapid cycling
  • Ensure medication stability before starting
  • Have someone check on you during the wake period
  • Antidepressant effects may be temporary without follow-up light therapy

Seasonal Adjustment Recommendations

Many people with BD have seasonal patterns. Adjusting chronotherapy seasonally helps maintain stability year-round.

Winter

Nov - Feb
  • Increase bright light therapy to 30-45 min daily
  • Use a dawn simulator alarm clock
  • Get outdoor light exposure during daylight hours, even on cloudy days
  • Watch for depressive symptoms as days shorten
  • Consider vitamin D supplementation (discuss with doctor)
  • Maintain consistent wake times despite shorter days

Spring

Mar - May
  • Watch for hypomania/mania as light increases rapidly
  • Reduce light therapy duration if mood elevates
  • Maintain evening dark therapy routine
  • Adjust bedtime if daylight starts disrupting sleep
  • Spring is the highest-risk season for mania onset
  • Increase monitoring of warning signs

Summer

Jun - Aug
  • May not need supplemental light therapy due to natural sunlight
  • Use blackout curtains to prevent early morning light wake-ups
  • Continue amber glasses in the evening
  • Stay alert for mania triggers from extended daylight
  • Maintain consistent bedtime despite late sunsets
  • Use sunglasses in bright midday light if mood elevating

Fall

Sep - Oct
  • Restart or increase light therapy as days shorten
  • Watch for depressive symptoms as daylight decreases
  • Adjust light therapy timing to match later sunrise
  • Prepare your winter chronotherapy plan with your care team
  • Daylight saving time change is a significant rhythm disruptor
  • Transition gradually -- do not change routines abruptly

Bright Light Therapy for Bipolar Depression

Strong Evidence

Multiple meta-analyses and systematic reviews support bright light therapy (BLT) for bipolar depression. The ISBD guidelines recommend BLT as an adjunctive treatment. Morning light at 10,000 lux for 15-30 minutes has shown significant antidepressant effects with NNT (number needed to treat) of approximately 4.

Sit DK, et al. Adjunctive Bright Light Therapy for Bipolar Depression: A Randomized Double-Blind Placebo-Controlled Trial. Am J Psychiatry. 2018;175(2):131-139.

Dark Therapy for Mania

Moderate Evidence

Studies show that extending the dark period to 14 hours (virtual darkness using amber glasses) can rapidly reduce manic symptoms. Henriksen et al. found that blue-blocking glasses worn from 6 PM reduced YMRS scores significantly more than placebo glasses within 3 days.

Henriksen TE, et al. Blue-blocking glasses as additive treatment for mania: a randomized placebo-controlled trial. Bipolar Disord. 2016;18(3):221-232.

Wake Therapy for Bipolar Depression

Strong Evidence

Total sleep deprivation produces rapid antidepressant effects in 40-60% of patients with bipolar depression, often within 24 hours. The effect can be sustained with concurrent bright light therapy and sleep phase advance (triple chronotherapy). Benedetti et al. found sustained response at 9 months with combined chronotherapy.

Benedetti F, et al. Rapid treatment response of suicidal symptoms to lithium, sleep deprivation, and light therapy (chronotherapeutics) in drug-resistant bipolar depression. J Clin Psychiatry. 2014;75(2):133-140.

Seasonal Patterns in BD

Established

Approximately 25% of people with bipolar disorder have a seasonal pattern. Mania peaks in spring/summer (associated with increasing daylight), while depression peaks in fall/winter. This pattern supports the use of light-based interventions timed to seasonal changes.

Geoffroy PA, et al. Seasonality and bipolar disorder: a systematic review. Bipolar Disord. 2014;16(8):803-811.

Chronotherapy should be conducted under the supervision of a healthcare provider. Wake therapy in particular requires clinical oversight. Do not attempt wake therapy without consulting your psychiatrist.
